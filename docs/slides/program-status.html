WHY THIS EXISTS
What We Set Out to Build
Agents + Apps + Lakebase
A lab centered on building real agentic apps: UC Functions as tools, Databricks Apps as the deployment target, Lakebase for conversation memory.
Full AgentOps Lifecycle
Not just building — evaluating and improving. MLflow tracing, eval datasets, LLM judges, scored eval runs, before-and-after proof.
Two Hours or a Full Day
Flexible delivery: standard 2-hour run with sample data, or stretched to a full day with a customer’s declared use case.
Vibe Coding as the Vehicle
Piggyback on customer interest in AI-assisted coding. Participants build entirely through prompting Claude via Coda — no raw code required.
Scripted (industry-specific scenarios) or customized (customer’s own use case). Same arc either way.

POSITIONING
Not Competing — Sequencing
Our Workshop
2-hour entry point. Customer builds an agent, runs evals, sees before/after scores. Surfaces readiness for the next step.
Path A — Vibe to Value
Customer needs more structure. They don’t know how to scope a use case or architect a solution. V2V provides the PRD, the prompts, and the guided path. Think: solution architecture for AI.
Path B — APJ Workshop-in-a-Box
Customer wants to understand how to build properly — not just vibe their way through it. They want confidence in the result. Traditional, production-grade, 1–2 days.
Our workshop creates the appetite. V2V and APJ satisfy it.
LESSONS LEARNED
What Made This Hard
1
Customer Environment Compatibility
Vibe coding requires tooling — Databricks CLI, AI Dev Kit — that can’t be assumed in customer environments. Answer: Coda. Native to Databricks, pre-installs everything needed, zero customer setup friction.

This also exposed a real issue: security practices around LiteLLM are blocking model calls in some environments. Hit it during build, verified it’s happening in customer environments this week. A fix is in motion.
2
Ownership and Scope Creep
The more we tried to share the load, the more the scope fragmented. Narrowed ownership to one person — hit the LiteLLM blocker. Relearned the lesson and consolidated further. Ended up primarily solo-built.

Distributed ownership of a workshop is harder than it looks.
3
Calibrating the Lab Itself
Getting the quality issues right — systematic enough to show up in evals, discoverable by participants, fixable within the time budget — took significant iteration.

Keeping the vibe coding arc natural without it devolving into hand-holding required careful design.
